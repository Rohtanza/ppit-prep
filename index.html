Case Study 1: The Zero-Dollar Clerical Error
Kyren (Company A) emails David (Tech One) a contract for support. Due to a clerical error, the hourly rate is listed as $000.00. David, sensing a promotion, signs and returns it. Company A never signs the document back. David spends his budget on new computers. When a technician later performs 2 hours of work, Company A sends an invoice for $200 and a “corrected” contract. David refuses to pay.
1. Is there an enforceable contract?
Likely not. A contract requires mutual intention and typically signatures from both parties to signify acceptance of terms. Furthermore, a rate of $000.00 may be viewed as lacking “Consideration” (each party must provide something of value).
2. Does David have a defense based on his expenditure?
He might attempt a claim based on reliance, but since Company A never signed the contract, his assumption of a “special promotion” without verification is risky.
3. What should Company A have done?
Under slide guidelines, they must ensure the final version is read clearly and signed by both parties before work begins.
Case Study 2: The “Hit by a Bus” Clause (Copyright)
A developer uses a large, proprietary shared-code base to build a small app for a client. The client suddenly demands the source code in the contract, fearing the developer might go out of business (“hit by a bus”).
1. Should the developer hand over the code?
No. Source code has value separate from the binary. Handing over a code base used for other products is a major IP risk.
2. What is a professional alternative?
Use a Software Code Escrow Service. This service holds the source code and only releases it to the client if the developer goes bankrupt or fails to maintain the software.
3. What legal principle applies to the code base ownership?
Copyright. As the author, the developer is the first owner. Unless the contract specifies “work for hire,” the developer retains rights to their pre-existing libraries.
Case Study 3: The Library Scan (Fair Use Analysis)
A professor scans entire journal articles and posts them on a password-protected site because students complained the library copies were always checked out.
1. Is this Fair Use?
Likely not. While the purpose is educational (nonprofit), the amount (entire articles) and the effect on the potential market (replacing the need for the library to buy more licenses or students to buy copies) weigh against fair use.
2. How could the professor make this legal?
Use excerpts rather than whole works, or obtain an open license/permission from the publisher.
3. Is the password protection a valid defense?
It limits the “Issue to the public” infringement, but the “Reproduction” right of the copyright holder is still violated by the act of scanning and hosting.
Case Study 4: Cyber Squatting vs. Trademark
An entrepreneur registers “FastDelivery-Service.com” before a famous company named “Fast Delivery” expands to the web. The entrepreneur offers to sell it to them for $50,000.
1. What is this practice called?
Cyber Squatting.
2. Which organization handles this dispute?
ICANN, through the Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy (UNDRP).
3. Why do trademarks and domain names conflict?
Trademarks are territorial and divided into classes (e.g., two companies can have the same name if one sells fruit and the other sells software). Domain names are unique, global, and “first come, first served.”
Case Study 5: The Secret Nanny Cam (Privacy)
The Sullivans install a laptop camera to monitor their nanny without informing her.
1. Is this ethically/legally sound?
No. Secret monitoring is an affront to human dignity. Privacy is a “zone of inaccessibility.”
2. What are the “Morally Acceptable Options”?
Conduct more comprehensive interviews, check references, observe from a distance in person, or be up-front about installing surveillance.
3. How does the “Right to be Let Alone” apply?
Based on Warren and Brandeis, individuals have a prudential right to privacy, especially in what they consider a secure working environment.
Case Study 6: Secondary Use of Medical Data
A hospital sells anonymized patient recovery data to a pharmaceutical company for research. Later, the company uses the data to identify and market drugs directly to those patients.
1. What principle of data protection is violated?
Secondary use. Data should only be used for the purpose for which it was collected.
2. What is the “Opt-in” vs “Opt-out” implication?
If the hospital used an “Opt-out” policy, they might claim legality, but ethical data protection suggests explicit “Opt-in” for sensitive medical data.
3. What happened in the Netflix Prize case that is relevant here?
Researchers proved that “anonymized” data can often be de-anonymized if other external data is available.
Case Study 7: Bespoke System Failure (The Faujisoft Scenario)
A company builds a custom MIS for a client. No formal requirements were written. The supplier wrote the contract, which the client signed without legal review. The software is delivered but is full of bugs and doesn’t meet needs.
1. Can the client claim damages?
In the Faujisoft example, the client could not claim anything because the supplier-friendly contract didn’t specify performance standards.
2. What should the “Acceptance Procedure” have included?
Criteria for judgment of successful completion and specific “Schedules/Annexes” of what was to be supplied.
3. What is the difference between Warranty and Maintenance here?
A warranty would have forced the supplier to fix the bugs for free. Maintenance would allow the client to request changes at a cost.
Case Study 8: Whistleblowing and Confidentiality
An IT consultant discovers their client is illegally dumping chemicals into a local river. The consultant signed an NDA.
1. Can the consultant report this?
Yes. An obligation of confidentiality is not absolute.
2. What is the legal justification?
Courts rule it is in the “Public Interest” to disclose confidential information regarding criminal offenses or environmental damage.
3. Does this rule out an action for breach of confidence?
Yes, public interest disclosure is a valid defense against a breach of confidence claim.
Long-Answer Essay Questions
1. The Relationship Between Law and Ethics: The Four-Quadrant Model

Question: Discuss the conceptual relationship between law and ethics. Explain how the four-quadrant model categorizes human actions and provide real-world examples for each quadrant as described in the source context.

Model Answer: Law and ethics are distinct but interconnected frameworks. Law consists of rules enforced by the state to regulate human conduct, ensure justice, and provide security. Ethics often serve as the foundation for law; for instance, the ethical principle of helping others leads to “Good Samaritan” laws. However, they do not always overlap perfectly. The four-quadrant model illustrates this relationship:

  • Quadrant I: Ethical and Legal: Actions that satisfy both moral standards and state regulations. Example: Firing an employee who fails to follow contractual obligations or meet performance expectations.
  • Quadrant II: Ethical but Not Legal: Actions that may be morally justifiable but violate specific laws. Example: Copying copyrighted software to use strictly as a backup, even when the license agreement specifically prohibits any copying.
  • Quadrant III: Unethical but Legal: Actions that are permitted by law but are considered morally wrong. Example: Failing to cite sources in a research paper or revealing data that was expected to remain confidential (but not protected by a formal contract).
  • Quadrant IV: Unethical and Not Legal: Actions that violate both moral principles and the law. Example: Planting viruses in a computer system or making unauthorized copies of copyrighted software for distribution/piracy.
2. Intellectual Property Protection for Software: Copyright vs. Patent

Question: Compare and contrast copyright and patent protection as they apply to software. Specifically, discuss the criteria for each and the status of software patentability within the Pakistani legal framework.

Model Answer: Copyright and patents offer different forms of protection for software:

  • Copyright: This protects the “expression” of an idea rather than the idea itself. It automatically applies to original literary works, which includes computer programs, source code, and documentation. Protection is automatic upon creation (registration is not required) and lasts for the author’s life plus 50 years. It grants the right to reproduce, distribute, and create derivative works.
  • Patents: These protect “inventions” (products or processes) that are novel, useful, and non-obvious. A patent grants a 20-year monopoly to the inventor. Unlike copyright, a patent must be applied for and granted by the state.
  • Comparison and Limitations: In many jurisdictions, copyright is the primary protection for software. In Pakistan, according to the Patents Ordinance of 2000, computer programs (software) are explicitly listed as un-patentable inventions. Software developers in Pakistan must rely on Copyright (under the Copyright Ordinance 1962), Trade Secrets, or Confidential Information (Non-Disclosure Agreements) to protect their intellectual property.
3. The Eight Fundamental Data Protection Principles

Question: Identify and explain the eight principles of data protection that govern the collection and processing of personal information.

Model Answer: Data protection laws, such as the UK Data Protection Acts of 1984 and 1998, are built on eight core principles to protect individuals from the misuse of their personal data:

  1. Fair and Lawful Processing: Data must be processed fairly, lawfully, and typically with the individual’s consent.
  2. Purpose Limitation: Data must be obtained for one or more specified, lawful purposes and not processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes.
  3. Adequacy and Relevance: Data must be adequate, relevant, and not excessive for the purpose it serves.
  4. Accuracy: Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date.
  5. Storage Limitation: Data should not be kept longer than is necessary for the stated purpose.
  6. Rights of Individuals: Data must be processed in accordance with the legal rights of the data subject.
  7. Security (Operational Impact): Appropriate technical and organizational measures (access controls, backups, integrity checks) must be taken against unauthorized processing or accidental loss.
  8. International Transfer Restrictions: Personal data should not be transferred to territories outside the original jurisdiction unless that territory ensures an adequate level of protection.
4. Privacy in the Digital Age: Tracking and Identification Technologies

Question: Discuss how modern technologies such as RFID tags, cookies, and facial recognition impact individual privacy. Explain the risks associated with the “electronic trail” left by these tools.

Model Answer: Technological advancements have made the collection and distribution of personal information easier, leading to the erosion of the “zone of inaccessibility.”

  • RFID Tags: These wireless transmitters are replacing barcodes. If they cannot be disabled, they become tracking devices that follow a consumer or product even after purchase.
  • Cookies and Flash Cookies: Browser cookies track visits to websites to personalize services. Flash cookies (Adobe) are more invasive, as they can hold 25 times more data and “respawn” deleted browser cookies, bypassing standard privacy controls.
  • Facial Recognition (Facebook Tags): This technology automatically suggests names for people in photos. Groups like EPIC have complained that this increases the risk of improper tagging and unauthorized identification.
  • Risks: These technologies create an “electronic trail.” Information like credit card purchases, E911 location data, and loyalty card habits are often sold or shared. This can lead to discrimination (e.g., an employer using medical/location data) or unauthorized surveillance.
5. Ethical Implications of Data Mining and Information Ownership

Question: Analyze the ethics of data mining, focusing on “secondary use” and the “opt-in vs. opt-out” debate. Provide examples of where data mining has faced public backlash.

Model Answer: Data mining involves analyzing large datasets to find patterns or predict preferences (Collaborative Filtering).

  • Secondary Use: This is an ethical concern where information collected for one purpose (e.g., search queries) is used for another (e.g., targeted advertising).
  • Ownership and Consent: The debate centers on who controls transaction information. Under Opt-in, organizations must get explicit permission to share data; under Opt-out, they can share data until the consumer forbids it. Marketing firms prefer Opt-out as it reduces barriers.
  • Public Backlash Examples:
    • Lotus Marketplace: A planned CD containing data on 120 million Americans was dropped after 30,000 privacy complaints.
    • Facebook Beacon: Originally an opt-out system that shared user purchases with friends, it was forced to become opt-in after widespread criticism.
    • Netflix Prize: Netflix released 100 million “anonymized” movie ratings, but researchers proved individuals could be re-identified, leading to a lawsuit and the cancellation of future contests.
6. Essential Components of Bespoke System Contracts

Question: Define “bespoke systems” and outline the critical terms and conditions that should be included in a fixed-price contract for their development.

Model Answer: Bespoke systems are tailor-made software solutions developed specifically for a client’s unique needs. A robust contract for such systems should include:

  • Requirements Specification: A clear definition of what is to be produced to avoid the system not meeting user needs (as seen in the Ministry of Information v. Faujisoft case).
  • Deliverables: Explicitly listing source code, documentation, manuals, test data, and training.
  • IP Rights: Defining who owns the final software and whether any shared libraries or pre-existing code remain the property of the developer.
  • Acceptance Procedure: Determining the criteria for “successful completion,” who conducts the tests, and what happens if they fail.
  • Warranty and Maintenance: Defining the period for free bug removal (Warranty) versus requested changes (Maintenance).
  • Penalty Clauses: Terms for delays caused by the supplier, though these often result in higher bid prices.
  • Termination and Arbitration: Provisions for how the contract ends and how disputes are settled (often through a neutral third party rather than litigation).
7. Fundamental Elements of a Legally Enforceable Contract

Question: According to contract law, what are the three essential elements required for a binding agreement? Explain each and discuss the role of warranties.

Model Answer: A contract is an agreement between two or more parties that is enforceable in court. The three essential elements are:

  1. Offer: An expression of a present intention to create a binding agreement.
  2. Acceptance: Agreement with the offer that “mirrors” the terms and is communicated in a reasonable manner.
  3. Consideration: A legal “detriment” where each party receives something of value and provides something in return.

Warranties are also vital terms:

  • Express Warranties: Specific promises made by the seller.
  • Implied Warranty of Merchantability: An assumption that the goods reasonably conform to an ordinary buyer’s expectations.
  • Implied Warranty of Fitness for a Particular Purpose: Applies when a buyer relies on the seller to select goods for a specific, stated request.

9. Freedom of Information and Its Conflict with Data Protection

Question: Explain the purpose of Freedom of Information (FOI) legislation and discuss why it sometimes conflicts with Data Protection laws.

Model Answer: The primary purpose of Freedom of Information (such as Pakistan’s Right to Information Act 2013) is to provide the public with clear rights of access to information held by public sector bodies. It promotes transparency and accountability in government.

The Conflict:

  • Data Protection is designed to keep personal information private and prevent unauthorized access.
  • Freedom of Information aims to make information public. Conflict arises when a requested public document contains personal data. Laws must balance the public’s “right to know” against an individual’s “right to privacy.” Legislation often includes enforcement mechanisms for FOI while providing specific exemptions for documents that would violate data protection if released.
10. Cyber Squatting and the UNDRP

Question: Define cyber squatting and explain the role of ICANN and the Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy (UNDRP) in addressing this issue.

Model Answer: Cyber Squatting is the practice of registering trademarks of other companies as domain names with the intent to sell them to the trademark owner at an inflated price. This is possible because domain names are registered on a “first come, first served” basis across all jurisdictions, whereas trademarks are territorial and class-based.

To combat this, ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) adopted the Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy (UNDRP). Recommended by WIPO, the UNDRP provides a streamlined legal framework to resolve disputes where a domain name is identical or confusingly similar to a trademark, the registrant has no legitimate interest in the name, and the name was registered in bad faith (such as for cyber squatting).
