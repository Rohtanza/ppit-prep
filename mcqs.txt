Comprehensive Study Guide: Legal Aspects, Intellectual Property, and Privacy in Computing

This study guide serves as a comprehensive synthesis of the legal and ethical frameworks governing the field of information technology. It covers foundational legal principles, intellectual property rights, data protection, privacy considerations, and the intricacies of computer contracts.

Part 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1. In the case of eBay v. Bidder's Edge, which legal concept was centrally applied regarding unauthorized data crawling? A. Trademark dilution B. Trespass to chattels C. Unjust enrichment D. Misappropriation Correct Answer: B

2. How is "Law" defined within the context of a state? A. A set of ethical suggestions for professional conduct B. A body of rules that can be enforced in a court of law C. International guidelines for human behavior D. Voluntarily adopted protocols for societal regulation Correct Answer: B

3. Which term refers to the specific geographical area or subject matter over which a single legal system and set of laws have authority? A. Legislation B. Precedent C. Jurisdiction D. Executive power Correct Answer: C

4. What is the primary psychological purpose of laws according to the provided materials? A. To encourage academic excellence B. To provide a sense of security and scare individuals into being "good" C. To replace the need for formal education D. To eliminate the need for a judicial system Correct Answer: B

5. Which branch of law characterizes certain wrongdoings as offenses against the State? A. Civil Law B. Private Law C. Criminal Law D. Law of Succession Correct Answer: C

6. A "Tort" is defined as a civil wrong where the remedy is a common law action for: A. Mandatory imprisonment B. Unliquidated damages C. Specific performance of a contract D. State-issued fines only Correct Answer: B

7. Which specific type of law determines the rules regulating the structure and relationships between principal organs of government, such as Federal and Provincial authorities? A. Administrative Law B. Constitutional Law C. Procedural Law D. Substantive Law Correct Answer: B

8. What is the difference between Substantive Law and Procedural Law? A. Substantive law defines rights and duties; Procedural law governs the steps to enforce them. B. Substantive law is for criminal cases; Procedural law is for civil cases. C. Substantive law is international; Procedural law is domestic. D. There is no difference; they are interchangeable terms. Correct Answer: A

9. In a federal state, which body is responsible for making new laws through representatives? A. The Judiciary B. The Executive C. The Legislature (Parliament) D. The Police Correct Answer: C

10. What type of jurisdiction is exercised when a court hears a case for the first time? A. Appellate Jurisdiction B. Pecuniary Jurisdiction C. Original Jurisdiction D. Territorial Jurisdiction Correct Answer: C

11. Between "General Law" and "Special Law," which one prevails in a legal conflict? A. General Law B. Special Law C. Neither; they must be merged D. The one decided by the Executive branch Correct Answer: B

12. "Common Law" is primarily based on: A. Acts of Parliament B. Written statutes C. Previous judgments of judges (Precedents) D. International treaties only Correct Answer: C

13. In the "Law and Ethics" quadrants, how is "Copying copyrighted software for a backup when prohibited by the agreement" categorized? A. Quadrant I: Ethical and Legal B. Quadrant II: Ethical but Not Legal C. Quadrant III: Unethical but Legal D. Quadrant IV: Not Ethical and Not Legal Correct Answer: B

14. In the "Law and Ethics" quadrants, "Planting viruses in someone else’s computer" is categorized as: A. Ethical but not legal B. Unethical but legal C. Not ethical and not legal D. Ethical and legal Correct Answer: C

15. What is the primary difference between Tangible and Intangible property? A. Tangible property cannot be stolen; intangible property can. B. Tangible property can be physically touched; intangible property is a product of the human intellect. C. Intangible property is protected by theft laws; tangible property is protected by IP laws. D. Tangible property has no value; intangible property is the "currency of the future." Correct Answer: B

16. Intellectual Property (IP) Rights should be viewed as: A. A single, indivisible law B. A package of different rights used to protect various aspects of a product C. Rules that only apply to printed books D. A voluntary system with no legal enforcement Correct Answer: B

17. Which type of IP protection is automatic once an original work is created in a fixed medium? A. Patent B. Trademark C. Copyright D. Industrial Design Correct Answer: C

18. In Copyright law, what is the "key" requirement for a work to qualify for protection? A. Usefulness B. Commercial value C. Originality D. Registration with the state Correct Answer: C

19. What is the standard duration of Copyright protection? A. 20 years from creation B. 50 years from creation C. Life of the author plus 50 years D. Indefinite, as long as it is used Correct Answer: C

20. Which of the following is NOT one of the five principal rights of a copyright owner? A. Right to reproduce the work B. Right to distribute copies C. Right to a monopoly on the underlying idea D. Right to perform the work in public Correct Answer: C

21. "Fair Use" allows copyrighted material to be used without permission primarily for: A. Commercial software redistribution B. Journalism, education, and research C. Creating a competing product D. Any purpose as long as it is on the internet Correct Answer: B

23. Which IP right requires an invention to be "novel, useful, and non-obvious"? A. Copyright B. Trademark C. Patent D. Confidential Information Correct Answer: C

25. Which of the following is UN-patentable in Pakistan? A. A new chemical process B. A mechanical invention C. Computer Programs (Software) D. Industrial machinery Correct Answer: C

26. A Trademark protects: A. The functional technical features of an invention B. Words, names, or symbols that distinguish the source of goods C. The source code of a software package D. Scientific and mathematical formulae Correct Answer: B

Part 2: Glossary of Key Terms

Term	Definition
Acceptance	Agreement with an offer in a manner reasonable under the circumstances; must mirror the offer.
Bespoke System	A software system custom-designed and built for a specific organization or user.
Civil Law	Also known as Private Law; concerned with rights and duties of individuals/organizations toward each other.
Collaborative Filtering	A data mining technique that predicts a user's interests by collecting preferences from many users.
Common Law	Traditional law based on judicial precedents rather than written statutes.
Consideration	Something of value (legal detriment) exchanged by parties that forms the basis of a contract.
Cookie	A small file placed on a hard drive by a web server to track visits and provide personalized services.
Copyright	An IP right protecting original literary, artistic, musical, or dramatic works from unauthorized copying.
Criminal Law	Law relating to offenses against the State; involves punishment ranging from fines to death.
Cyber Squatting	The bad-faith registration of a domain name that is a trademark of another entity.
Data Mining	The process of discovering patterns and knowledge from large amounts of data.
Fair Use	A legal doctrine allowing limited use of copyrighted material without permission for specific purposes.
Flash Cookie	A data file stored via Adobe Flash that can hold more data than standard cookies and "respawn" them.
Industrial Design	Protection for the ornamental or aesthetic aspect of an article (shape, patterns, lines, or color).
Intellectual Property	Intangible property that is the product of human intellect, such as inventions and artistic works.
Jurisdiction	The legal authority of a court or entity to administer justice within defined limits (territory, subject, or value).
Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)	A contract specifically intended to establish legal obligations of confidence.
Opt-in	A policy where a consumer must explicitly give permission before their information can be shared.
Patent	A government-granted monopoly for a limited period (usually 20 years) for a novel and useful invention.
Public Domain	Works that are not protected by IP rights and are free for anyone to use.
RFID	Radio Frequency Identification; uses electromagnetic fields to automatically identify and track tags attached to objects.
Statute Law	Written law passed by a legislative body (e.g., Acts of Parliament).
Tort	A civil wrong (excluding breach of contract) for which the court provides a remedy in damages.
Trademark	A recognizable sign, design, or expression which identifies products or services of a particular source.
TRIPS	Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights; an international agreement between WTO members.
